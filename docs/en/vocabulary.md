# Vocabulary

Vocabulary is the building block of language. When learning a language, you must start with the basics. Without a foundation, you cannot learn better, faster, or more easily.

---

## Why Vocabulary Matters

**Vocabulary has a significant characteristic of quantitative change leading to qualitative change.** When the number of words you master reaches a tipping point, you'll find that learning English becomes incredibly easy.

Thanks to contextual help, you can infer the meaning of most new words, allowing you to use better original English learning materials to enhance your learning, such as The Economist and English novels.

---

## Vocabulary Distribution

![OEC Vocabulary Distribution](/assets/OCE%20Artboard@2x.png)

From the Oxford English Corpus (OEC) data:

- **1,000** words can help you understand about **75%** of English content
- **7,000** words is a critical tipping point (just 7,000 words can help you understand nearly 90% of content)
- The return on memorizing words decreases over time

**Small goal**: Try 7,000 words first. Break it down: 40 words per day, without forgetting, it only takes 175 days!

## Visual vs. Auditory Learners

Before expanding your vocabulary, take some time to understand your learning characteristics.

### Visual Learners

Visual learners memorize words through _visualization_, such as binding words with _images/videos_, or even seeing the word itself as a _visual graphic/symbol_.

**Suggestion**: Visual memorization should use _abstract_ methods, visualizing the word's corresponding information as something you understand.

### Auditory Learners

Auditory learners have a natural advantage in language learning. Learning through大量 _listening_ is closer to natural language acquisition.

**Suggestion**: Auditory learners must master _pronunciation_ knowledge. Once you have a good _pronunciation_ foundation, you can convert most of what you hear into correctly spelled words.

> Whether you are a visual or auditory learner, when learning words, whenever possible, please use a dictionary to listen to the word's pronunciation!

## Two Methods of Memorizing Words

### Method 1: Daily Moderate Memorization

**Pros:**
- Flexible time allocation (suitable for using fragmented time like commuting, before bed)
- Light burden
- Not easy to produce frustration
- Suitable for learning core vocabulary

**Cons:**

Since the return on memorizing words decreases and there is usually no effective positive feedback, the reality is often that people give up after memorizing 800 words, 1,000 words, or even 1,001 words.

### Method 2: Massive Input

By memorizing a large number of words and reviewing quickly, although the forgetting rate is higher than moderate memorization, you can win by quantity.

For example: Memorize 20 words a day, 600 words a month. Assuming 80% form long-term memory, you'll remember 480 words.

But if I use the same time to quickly look at 100 words a day, 3,000 words a month. Assuming 30% are remembered, you'll remember 900 words—far more than the first method.

This is suitable for the learning stage of 1,000~7,000 vocabulary.

::: tip Note
When your vocabulary exceeds 7,000, you should have the skill to learn "new words" through morphology and context.
:::

## Word Memorization Tips

- **Repetition, repetition, repetition** (No other way, just practice makes perfect)
- **Use sticky notes** (Place words where you can see them often, glance occasionally)
- **Root and affix method** (Learn common roots and affixes, extrapolate)
- **Context memorization** (Memorize words in sentences and articles)
- **Spaced repetition** (Use the forgetting curve, scientifically arrange review time)
